Methodology: Results are from a retrospective pre-post evaluation (n=37, March 2026). Respondents self-rated "before the project" and "now" in a single session on a 1–5 scale. This design is practical for field settings but carries recall bias risk. There is no control group. The male subsample (n=10) limits the reliability of gender comparisons; these are reported as descriptive patterns, not confirmed effects.

HEC finding: 65% of respondents have been affected by wild elephants; 32% farm "very close" to the protected forest. "I feel safe from elephant danger" showed the largest attitude shift (+0.92). Gender patterns in HEC: Gender differences are small and inconsistent. Women showed slightly more on safety (+0.96 vs +0.80) and adequacy of support (+0.44 vs +0.00). Men showed slightly more on trust in authorities (+0.40 vs +0.19) and conservation involvement (+0.40 vs +0.22). HEC Attitudes Change by Exposure Status

AttitudeAffected ChangeNot Affected ChangeAffected BeforeAffected NowNot Affected BeforeNot Affected Now
Exposure patterns in HEC: Affected farmers showed systematically smaller attitudinal gains on 5 of 7 items. The starkest divergence: trust in authorities (+0.04 affected vs +0.62 not affected) and adequacy of support (+0.12 vs +0.69). The one exception is safety from elephants, where affected gained more (+1.04 vs +0.69), likely reflecting direct benefit from HEC safety training. Affected farmers do not trust institutions more, do not feel adequately supported, and do not see elephants as beneficial.

E. Most Valuable Benefits Respondents selected up to 3

Top benefits: "Learning new skills" was selected by 78% of respondents, making it the clear standout. "Higher prices" and "Exchange visits" tied at 49%, followed by "Improved quality / Fine Robusta" at 38%. Notably, "Reduced elephant stress" was selected by only 5%, consistent with the modest HEC attitude improvements.
